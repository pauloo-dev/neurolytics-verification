1
Teachers put together small bundles of changes
Theme 1: Adaptation Ensembles
▶
Summary
When a lesson is not working for a learner, teachers in this study made several small adjustments at the same time. For example, a teacher might move a child closer to their desk, switch briefly to Kiswahili, break the task into smaller steps, and pair the child with a helpful classmate, all in the same lesson. These combinations of small changes are things teachers work out from experience and close observation. They also tend to be low-cost, that is, they do not require special equipment or extra budget. The choice of which combination to use seems to be driven by what the teacher knows about the learner and what is practical in that moment.

Q3. Is there anything you would like to add or change in this summary?
2
Teachers assess success through small signs. Not just test scores
Theme 2: Inclusive Utility (Presence, Participation, Progression)
▶
Summary
When I asked teachers how they know whether what they are doing is working, almost no one mentioned exam results first. Instead, they talked about smaller and more immediate signs. For instance; A child who was absent a lot suddenly starts coming every day. A child who never puts up their hand starts trying to answer. A child who could not copy a single word from the board attempts two or three. These are small milestones and feel just as significant as a grade. Sometimes more so. Teachers also talked about belonging: whether the child seems comfortable in the room and accepted by classmates, and whether they feel like they are part of what is happening in the lesson. For some learners, especially those in special needs settings, the definition of progress stretched well beyond curriculum content.
Q1. Does this capture how you personally judge whether your efforts are working?
Q2. Are there other signs of progress you look for that are not mentioned here?
Q3. Is there anything here that feels inaccurate or that you would describe differently?
3
The system makes it very hard, and it was designed for a different kind of school
Theme 3: Structural and Ecological Tensions
▶
Summary
Teachers across all school settings described significant barriers that limit what they can do for learners who need more support. Class sizes is one of them and regularly exceed 60 or 70. Government tablets are broken and outdated. Many schools don't have Wi-Fi, so teachers use their own phones and pay for their own data to access learning materials. The CBE was partly modelled on education systems in countries like Finland, where there are specialist support teachers in every class and class sizes of around 18 to 21 learners. None of those conditions exists in most Kenyan public schools. Teachers described carrying these gaps largely alone, finding ways to work around them with whatever is available or affordable. The pressure of high-stakes exams also continues to push many teachers back toward exam-focused teaching, even as the curriculum officially promotes competencies.
Q1. Do these barriers match what you experience in your school?
Q2. Are there any important barriers missing from this summary?
Q3. Is there anything here you would describe differently?
4
Teachers rely on each other informally, WhatsApp
Theme 4: Lateral Collaboration
▶
Summary
When teachers need support, whether to understand a learner better, find a resource, or figure out how to explain something, they usually turn to a trusted colleague. WhatsApp groups among teachers are the most commonly mentioned platform: teachers share resources, and sometimes solve problems together through these groups. Subject panel meetings also happen, but they tend to be irregular and often do not lead to changes in classroom practice. Where two teachers share a classroom, as happens in a couple of the schools in this study, that arrangement makes a big difference. Having a second person who can support individual learners while the lesson continues changes what is possible. Parental involvement varies greatly: helpful in some urban settings, resistant or absent in many rural ones.
Q1. Does this reflect the kind of support and teamwork that actually happens in your school?
Q2. Is there a form of support or teamwork that is missing from this picture?
Q3. Anything you would change or add?
5
Teachers do all of this because they care deeply, and it costs them personally
Theme 5: Teacher Adaptive Agency as Moral Vocation
▶
Summary
One of the most striking things across all nine conversations was how much teachers invest beyond what is expected. We have teachers arriving before 6:30am, others come in on Saturdays without pay, give up lunch breaks for individual sessions with learners who are struggling, and even spend their own money on materials, printed worksheets, and mobile data for accessing teaching resources. When I asked why, nobody gave a professional obligation as the reason. They talked about caring for the child, not being able to set aside knowing a learner has not understood, and teaching being something that comes from the heart. This dedicated personal investment is what keeps inclusive practice alive in conditions that could otherwise justify giving up. But it also means the system is relying heavily on individual teachers in ways that are not sustainable and that actually hide the need for proper structural support.
Q1. Does this feel true to what you described about why you teach the way you do?
Q2. Is there anything about the personal cost that feels overstated or understated?
Q3. Is there anything you would like to add?
6
Teachers clearly see learner differences, but get almost no professional support to respond to them
Theme 6: Neurodivergence Recognition
▶
Summary
Every teacher in this study described learners with noticeable differences in how they learn: difficulties with reading, writing, copying from the board, following verbal instructions, or engaging socially in the classroom. Some teachers named these directly, using terms like autism, dyslexia, or dyscalculia. Others described the behaviours in detail without using clinical labels. In almost every case, there was no formal diagnosis, no specialist support. Teachers figured out what they could by themselves: moving the child closer, switching to Kiswahili, giving a different kind of task. Several teachers also noticed what learners were good at, for example drawing, carpentry, music, or sports, and used these strengths as a starting point for the lesson. Researchers who study neurodiversity say that this is exactly what schools should do, even though none of the teachers had been trained in that approach.
Q1. Does this match your experience of identifying and responding to learners who learn differently?
Q2. Is the referral process described here accurate to what you have experienced?
Q3. Is there anything important that is missing from this summary?
7
The CBE is a good idea that has not yet become a reality for most learners
Theme 7: The CBE Paradox
▶
Summary
Without exception, every teacher in this study expressed genuine support for what the CBE is trying to do: move away from rote learning and towards competency, allow learners to be recognised for different kinds of ability, and create pathways based on strength rather than a single exam result. And without exception, every teacher also described a significant gap between that vision and what is actually happening in schools. The high-stakes exam still determines transition, so teachers still teach for the exam. The cluster school system still ranks schools, so parents still push for the top schools. The pathway system that was supposed to send learners to schools matching their talents often leads to schools that lack the very facilities needed for those pathways. Teachers described living in what one called a "hybrid system": officially CBE, but structurally still shaped by the logic of the system it was meant to replace.
Q1. Does this capture the tensions you experience between what the CBE promises and what you can actually deliver?
Q2. Is there anything about the CBE or the system around it that feels misrepresented or missing?
Q3. Is there anything you would like to change or add?
