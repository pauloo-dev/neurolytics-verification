Analytic Memos

Five session memos documenting key interpretive decisions, analytic surprises, and rationale for coding choices. These constitute the reflexive journalling component of the audit trail.

Memo 1 — First Read-Through · All 9 transcripts
Initial familiarisation with the full corpus. Struck immediately by the consistency of the personal cost narrative across very different teachers — T01 (urban, senior school) and T08 (rural, marginal community) share the same willingness to absorb financial and temporal costs without institutional recognition. This feels latent — not something teachers explicitly flag as remarkable, but something that saturates the data. Note: the 6:30am/Saturday detail from T01 needs a latent code, not just a semantic one. The semantic code would be "unpaid extra time" but the latent dimension is something about moral vocation that precedes and exceeds the specific behaviour. Will flag this in v2.

Also: T03's account of the boy who cannot copy from the board is analytically rich. Three years of sustained, unresolved effort. The absence of diagnostic support is made visible not through complaint but through the flatness of the description — "I don't know what to do." This is a teacher who has reached the edge of their adaptive capacity without any institutional response. Strong candidate for a quote in Ch4 under both Theme 5 (moral vocation) and Theme 6 (identification without diagnosis).
Memo 2 — First Coding Pass · Semantic Level
Semantic coding generated 47 initial codes (Codebook v1). The challenge at this stage is the "adaptation" codes — there are too many that are conceptually close but practically distinct. "Flashcards," "wall charts," "projector," and "pictures from internet" are all doing similar analytic work (making content visible) but arise from very different resourcing contexts. Decided to group these as visual_multimodal in v2, while retaining the distinction in the coding spreadsheet extract notes because the resourcing dimension is analytically important for Theme 3.

T04's data stands apart from the rest: the IEP, the modelling clay, the gadgets from KISE, the chicken coop. The SNE context produces a qualitatively different kind of adaptation. Need to be careful not to let T04 pull Theme 1 too far toward specialist practice — the coding should reflect what is being compared against mainstream contexts.
Memo 3 — Latent Coding · Values Dimension
Moved to latent coding pass after semantic coding was complete. Three latent codes emerged that were not visible at the semantic level: cooperation_vs_collaboration (Theme 4), CBE_goodwill_despite_constraints (Theme 7), and vocation_heart (Theme 5). The first two required drawing on the literature — the Harris (2013) distinction between cooperation and collaboration is not something any participant uses explicitly, but it maps precisely onto what they describe. The "goodwill despite critique" code captures something T02 says directly ("I support it 100%") and T05 implies ("the government is not ready but the curriculum itself is good"). This double-consciousness of systemic critique and personal commitment is a latent pattern that no individual participant articulates but that emerges structurally across accounts.

Concern: am I importing too much from Harris (2013) at the expense of participants' own categories? Counter-check: ran back through T04, T07, T08 data — none describe what they do as "collaboration" in the proper sense. T07 is closest (co-teacher model) but explicitly notes role ambiguity. The latent code holds.
Memo 4 — Theme Construction · Consolidation
Theme construction from 22 focused codes (v2) to 7 themes. The most difficult decision was whether Theme 5 (Moral Vocation) should be a standalone theme or absorbed into Theme 1 (Adaptation Ensembles) as its motivational substrate. Argument for absorption: moral vocation is what produces adaptation ensembles. Argument against: it is analytically distinct — it explains the sustainability of adaptive behaviour, not the form it takes, and it speaks directly to RQ1's "pedagogical reasoning" component. Decision: keep separate. The tension between the two themes is itself analytically productive for Ch4.

Theme 6 (Neurodivergence Recognition) was at risk of being a thin theme — only 5 codes. But the strength-based framing code is carrying heavy latent-level analytical work (the spontaneous turn toward neurodiversity paradigm without any training in it) and the parent denial code is essential for understanding why formal support pathways break down. Retained as standalone with a note that it is the "condition" that motivates the adaptations documented in Theme 1.
Memo 5 — Counter-Example Check · Disconfirming Cases
Systematic check for counter-examples and disconfirming cases before finalising themes. Key disconfirming cases noted:

Against Theme 4 (Collaboration): T05 explicitly states that schemes of work are done for inspection compliance, not genuine planning ("If nobody's coming to check them, we don't do that"). This directly disconfirms any claim that formal collaboration structures are functioning as intended. Incorporated into the cooperation/collaboration latent code as the sharpest available counter-evidence.

Against Theme 2 (Inclusive Utility — Presence): T05 is the only participant to report actual dropout (~2%). All other teachers report near-100% attendance. This makes T05 a crucial analytic outlier — not evidence against Theme 2 but evidence that the presence P is contextually variable in ways urban school data alone would obscure. T05's account strengthens rather than weakens the theme by showing that presence is not guaranteed and has identifiable causes when it fails.

Against Theme 7 (CBE goodwill): T08 is the most sceptical of CBE rhetoric — "serious disconnect is there." But even T08 does not reject the principle; he rejects the implementation. The double-consciousness pattern holds. No genuine counter-evidence to Theme 7 found.

1
Teachers put together small bundles of changes, not single big ones
Theme 1 — Adaptation Ensembles
What came through very clearly in our conversations is that when a lesson is not working for a learner, teachers do not just make one change — they make several small adjustments at the same time. Maybe they move the child closer to their desk, switch briefly to Kiswahili, break the task into smaller steps, and pair the child with a helpful classmate — all in the same lesson. These bundles of small changes are things teachers work out from experience and observation, not things they are taught specifically. They are also low-cost: they do not require special equipment or extra budget. What drives the choice of which bundle to use seems to be a combination of what the teacher knows about the learner, what is practical in the moment, and what the classroom conditions allow.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this match what you described in our conversation?
  • Is there anything about how you make these decisions that you feel is missing or not quite right?
  • Is there anything you would like to add or change?
2
Teachers judge success through small signs, not just test scores
Theme 2 — Inclusive Utility (3P)
When I asked teachers how they know whether what they are doing is working, almost no one mentioned exam results first. Instead, they talked about smaller, more immediate signs: a child who was absent a lot suddenly starts coming every day; a child who never put up their hand starts trying to answer; a child who could not copy a single word from the board manages two or three. These small milestones feel just as significant — sometimes more significant — than a grade. Teachers also talked about belonging: whether the child seems comfortable in the room, whether classmates accept them, and whether they feel like they are part of what is happening in the lesson. For learners in special needs settings especially, the definition of "progress" stretched well beyond curriculum content.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this capture how you personally judge whether your efforts are working?
  • Are there other signs of progress you look for that are not mentioned here?
  • Is there anything that feels inaccurate?
3
The system makes it very hard — and it was designed for a different kind of school
Theme 3 — Structural-Ecological Tensions
Teachers across all school contexts described significant structural barriers that limit what they can do for learners who need more support. Class sizes regularly exceed 60 or 70. Government-issued tablets are broken or outdated. Many schools have no Wi-Fi, and teachers use their own phones and pay for their own data to access learning materials online. The CBE was modelled partly on systems like Finland's, where there are specialist support teachers alongside every class teacher, class sizes of 18–21, and fully equipped schools. None of those conditions exist in most Kenyan public schools. Teachers described carrying these gaps largely alone, improvising with whatever they could find or buy. The pressure of the KJSEA examination also continues to push many teachers back toward exam-focused teaching, even as the curriculum officially promotes competency-based learning.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Do these barriers match what you experience in your school?
  • Are there any important barriers missing from this summary?
  • Is there anything here you would describe differently?
4
Teachers rely on each other informally — WhatsApp more than meetings
Theme 4 — Lateral Collaboration
When teachers need support — whether to understand a learner better, find a resource, or figure out how to explain a concept — they usually turn to a trusted colleague, not a formal structure. WhatsApp groups among teachers are the most commonly mentioned platform: teachers share resources, ask questions, and sometimes solve problems together through these groups. Subject panel meetings also happen, but they tend to be irregular and often do not result in changes to classroom practice. Where two teachers share a classroom (as in a couple of the schools in this study), the arrangement makes a big difference — having a second person in the room who can support individual learners while the lesson continues changes what is possible. Parental involvement varies greatly: supportive in some urban contexts, resistant or absent in many rural ones.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this reflect the collaboration that actually happens in your school?
  • Is there a form of support or teamwork that is missing from this picture?
  • Anything you would change or add?
5
Teachers do all of this because they care deeply — and it costs them personally
Theme 5 — Teacher Adaptive Agency as Moral Vocation
One of the most striking things across all nine conversations was how much teachers invest beyond what is expected or compensated. Teachers arrive before 6:30am, come in on Saturdays without pay, give up lunch breaks for individual sessions with struggling learners, and spend their own money on materials, printed worksheets, and mobile data for accessing teaching resources. When I asked why, nobody gave a professional obligation — they talked about caring for the child, about not being able to sleep knowing a learner has not understood, about teaching being something that comes from the heart. This deep personal investment is what keeps inclusive practice alive in conditions that could otherwise justify giving up. But it also means the system relies heavily on individual teachers' goodwill in ways that are not sustainable and that mask the need for structural support.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this feel true to what you described about why you teach the way you do?
  • Is there anything about the personal cost that you feel is overstated or understated?
  • Is there anything you would like to add?
6
Teachers see learner differences clearly — but get almost no professional support to respond to them
Theme 6 — Neurodivergence Recognition
Every teacher in this study described learners with significant differences in how they learn — difficulties with reading, writing, copying from the board, following verbal instructions, or engaging socially in the classroom. Some teachers named these directly: autism, dyslexia, dyscalculia. Others described the behaviours in detail without using clinical labels. In almost every case, there was no formal diagnosis, no specialist support, and no referral pathway that actually moved quickly enough to help. Teachers figured out what they could by themselves: moving the child closer, translating into Kiswahili, giving them a different kind of task. Several teachers also noticed what learners were good at — drawing, carpentry, music, sports — and used these strengths as entry points into the lesson. This instinct to start from strength rather than deficit is exactly what research on neurodiversity says schools should do, even though none of the teachers had training in that framework.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this match your experience of identifying and responding to learners who learn differently?
  • Is the referral process described here accurate to what you have experienced?
  • Is there anything important missing?
7
The CBE is a good idea that has not yet become a reality for most learners
Theme 7 — The CBE Paradox
Without exception, every teacher in this study expressed genuine support for what the CBE is trying to do — move away from rote learning and toward competency, allow learners to be recognised for different kinds of ability, and create pathways based on strength rather than a single exam result. And without exception, every teacher also described a significant gap between that vision and what is actually happening in schools. The KJSEA examination still determines transition, so teachers still teach for the exam. The cluster school system still ranks schools, so parents still push for the "top" schools. The pathway system that was supposed to channel learners to schools matching their talents often leads to schools that lack the very facilities needed for those pathways. Teachers described living in a "hybrid system" — officially CBE, but structurally still shaped by the logic of the 8-4-4 it was meant to replace.

Questions for your reflection:

  • Does this capture the tensions you experience between what the CBE promises and what you can deliver in your classroom?
  • Is there anything about the system that feels misrepresented or missing?
  • Is there anything you would like to change?
