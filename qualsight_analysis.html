T1
Adaptation Ensembles: Assembling the Feasible Frontier
Sensitising lenses: UDL · Social Constructivism · Inclusive Utility
Teachers do not apply single adaptations in isolation. Rather, they assemble context-specific bundles — here termed adaptation ensembles — that combine low-cost, low-risk pedagogical adjustments to maximise Presence, Participation, and Progression (3P). These ensembles are shaped by the teacher's reading of learner need in real time, and calibrated to what is feasible within structural constraints. Key components include heterogeneous grouping, translanguaging, visual/multimodal mediation, task chunking, peer scaffolding, and flexible pacing. Crucially, these are not mandated by the curriculum or training — they emerge from teachers' situated pedagogical judgement.

T2
Inclusive Utility in Practice: Reading the 3Ps
Sensitising lenses: Inclusive Utility (3P framework) · IPAA (Florian, 2014)
Teachers across contexts demonstrate a working awareness of the three-dimensional nature of inclusion — Presence, Participation, and Progression — even when they do not use this terminology explicitly. However, what counts as evidence of each P is contested and context-dependent. Presence is near-universal in urban schools but fragile in rural contexts (seasonal attendance, economic pull-out). Participation is described behaviourally but often harder to assess for learners with autism or cognitive differences. Progression is most often read through small wins and observable behavioural change, not formal scores — marking an implicit departure from examination-based metrics and a tentative alignment with CBE's competency logic.
Presence (attendance patterns) Participation (active engagement) Progression (milestones/assessment) Celebrating small wins Belonging as pedagogical goal Dropout/disengagement

T3
Structural-Ecological Tensions: The Borrowed Architecture Problem
Sensitising lenses: Ecological Systems Theory · UDL (structural prerequisites) · Policy Borrowing (Phillips & Ochs, 2003)
Kenya's CBE was structured after Nordic competency-based models that assume a tripartite support system (class teacher + learning support teacher + classroom assistant), small class sizes (18–21), and universal assistive technology. None of these conditions are present in the study's Kenyan classrooms, where class sizes range from 31 to 90, assistive technology is almost entirely absent, and teachers routinely use their own smartphones and personal data bundles to access digital resources. This structural mismatch — what Florian (2014) warns is "inclusion-speak" — creates a compounding tension between CBE's aspirational design logic and its enacted reality. The contradiction is sharpest in the clustering system, which continues to reproduce merit-based stratification while the curriculum publicly disavows it.

T4
Lateral Collaboration as Lifeline: Cooperation Without Infrastructure
Sensitising lenses: Ecological Systems (mesosystem) · Collaboration for Inclusion (Mulholland & O'Connor, 2016)
In the absence of the vertically structured tripartite support assumed by the Nordic model, Kenyan teachers default to lateral, peer-to-peer collaboration as their primary professional lifeline. WhatsApp groups — described by all participants who addressed collaboration — serve as the de facto resource-sharing and problem-solving infrastructure. Subject panels, co-teaching arrangements (found in two schools), and informal cross-school networks supplement this. However, following the framework of Harris (2013) and Torres (2019), what is described is largely cooperation rather than collaboration: the structures lack joint problem-solving, role clarity, reciprocal accountability, and distributed leadership. Nevertheless, collaboration clearly functions as an efficient frontier shifter — expanding teachers' adaptation options at low cost.
WhatsApp (lateral, informal) Subject panels Co-teaching model Parental engagement (mixed) KICD/TSC retooling Cross-school zone collaboration SNE teacher as resource Community resources (church)

T5
Teacher Adaptive Agency as Moral Vocation
Sensitising lenses: VBE (Ngwacho, 2024) · DisCrit (Annamma et al., 2012) · Teacher Agency (Cong-Lem, 2021)
Across all nine participants, adaptation for inclusion is framed not as professional obligation but as moral calling — what T04 describes as "ni moyo" (it is the heart). Teachers routinely invest personal time, personal finances, personal data bundles, and emotional labour beyond any institutional expectation or compensation. This motivational substrate is what sustains adaptation ensembles in the absence of structural support. However, this reliance on individual moral vocation is simultaneously a strength and a systemic risk: it masks structural deficits, is not scalable, and places unsustainable burden on individual teachers, particularly those in under-resourced rural schools.

T6
Neurodivergence: Between Recognition, Resourcelessness, and the Strength-Turn
Sensitising lenses: Neurodiversity paradigm (Armstrong, 2012) · Social constructivism · DisCrit
Teachers demonstrate heterogeneous capacity to identify and respond to neurodivergent learners. All participants describe learners who present with features of autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia, or sensorimotor processing difficulties — yet almost none have received formal diagnostic support. Teachers are left to identify, assess, and respond without specialist support, using observation, intuition, and informal consultation with SNE colleagues. A significant emergent finding is a spontaneous, partial shift toward strength-based framing across participants: teachers consistently describe identifying and leveraging learner strengths (art, sports, music, oral communication) as an adaptive strategy — prefiguring the neurodiversity paradigm's core claim that difference, not deficit, is the starting point.
Identification without diagnosis Deficit vs strength framing Parent denial/resistance Medical model referral Strength-based observation Labels and their effects

T7
The CBE Paradox: Aspirational Design, Enacted Contradiction
Sensitising lenses: Policy Borrowing · Ecological Systems (macro-level) · VBE
Teachers express broad goodwill toward CBE's inclusive aspirations while simultaneously documenting a persistent implementation gap. The curriculum's declared learner-centredness and competency orientation are endorsed, but its enacted form remains entangled with the logics of the 8-4-4 system it was designed to replace — examination pressure, merit-based stratification, and content overload persist. Participants are navigating a "hybrid system" (T01's term) that officially promotes inclusion but structurally still rewards ranking. The pathway system — designed to leverage learner strengths — is widely identified as good policy but encountered as non-functioning in practice, particularly for learners in rural Cluster 4 contexts where even the designated pathway schools lack basic facilities.
Aspirational vs enacted gap Hybrid system tensions Examination pressure persistence Pathway promise vs reality Parental 8-4-4 mindset CBE goodwill vs structural unreadiness

1 — Adaptation Ensembles
13 codes
ADAPT :: heterogeneous_grouping
Teacher deliberately mixes learners of different ability levels within groups, rejecting streaming in favour of peer-assisted collaboration. Explicitly framed as countering competitive ranking.
9/9
ADAPT :: translanguaging
Use of Kiswahili, Kikuyu, or other languages to scaffold concepts taught/assessed in English. Emerges as individual initiative, not policy, and is sometimes hidden from colleagues/administration.
5/9
ADAPT :: visual_multimodal
Use of projectors, printed pictorials, wall charts, drawings, videos (often downloaded at own expense) to supplement verbal instruction for visual learners and those with language barriers.
7/9
ADAPT :: task_chunking
Breaking down a lesson or task into smaller sequential units. Includes simplified instructions, step-by-step delivery, and reducing question complexity for individual learners.
8/9
ADAPT :: peer_pairing
Deliberate pairing of a struggling learner with a peer who understands, enabling social scaffolding within the regular classroom without extracting the learner.
7/9
ADAPT :: seating_proximity
Positioning of neurodivergent or struggling learners close to the teacher's desk or away from windows/doors, used as a primary low-cost spatial accommodation.
6/9
ADAPT :: scaffolding_ido_wedo_youdo
Gradual release of responsibility framework: teacher models, then co-constructs, then releases. Named explicitly by T03 and referenced implicitly by others as an established pedagogical frame.
6/9
ADAPT :: flexible_extra_time
Allowing learners to complete tasks after the lesson period, granting oral alternatives to timed written work, or doubling the time allocation for timed reading tests.
7/9
ADAPT :: oral_alternatives
Allowing written-task completion through verbal response, dictation, or drawing, as an accommodation for learners with writing/motor difficulties.
4/9
ADAPT :: practical_handson
Use of real-world activities (chicken coops, cooking, field trips, objects from everyday life) to make abstract concepts concrete, particularly for learners who struggle with text-based tasks.
8/9
ADAPT :: music_songs
Using songs, rhymes, or musical patterns as a mnemonic and engagement tool. Notably used in poetry lessons (T02) and described as highly effective for reluctant/struggling learners.
4/9
ADAPT :: modelling_clay_objects
Physical manipulation of objects (clay, number shapes, letter forms, containers, bottle tops) to develop fine motor skills and make abstract symbols tangible for foundational learners.
3/9
ADAPT :: ICT_personal_phone
Teacher's personal smartphone used to search, download, or display digital content (YouTube, KICD portal) in the absence of school-provided technology. Represents a hidden personal cost.

2 — Inclusive Utility (3P)
6 codes
3P :: presence
Physical attendance and sense of belonging. Urban schools report near-100% attendance; rural schools report seasonal variability driven by farm labour and poverty. Belonging is framed as affective presence, not merely physical.
9/9
3P :: participation
Active engagement with lesson content, materials, or peers. Marked by hand-raising, completing activities, joining group work. Presence ≠ participation — several teachers note learners physically present but mentally disengaged.
9/9
3P :: progression
Forward movement, however small. Most teachers measure progression through small observable wins (new word read, extra sentence written, skill mastered) rather than test scores, signalling a de facto alignment with CBE's competency logic.
9/9
3P :: celebrating_small_wins
Explicit affirmation and celebration of micro-milestones as motivational and relational tools. Strongest in T04 (SNE) and T09 (Grade 1), but present across all levels.
6/9
3P :: belonging
Emotional safety and social acceptance as preconditions for participation. Teachers describe creating environments where learners "feel part and parcel" before expecting academic engagement.
7/9
3P :: dropout_disengagement
Withdrawal from schooling (physical or psychological). Rural contexts see higher rates; reasons include curriculum inaccessibility (T05: ~2%), poverty-driven pull-out (T08), and silent withdrawal from content-heavy Grade 9.
3/9
3 — Structural-Ecological Conditions
9 codes
STRUCT :: class_size
Class sizes range from 31 (SNE) to 90 (T01, lower primary). All mainstream participants report overcrowding as a primary constraint on individualised instruction. The Nordic-model baseline of 18–21 is never approached.
9/9
STRUCT :: resource_scarcity
Absence of basic materials (lab equipment, assistive devices, printing facilities, supplementary learning materials). Teachers routinely improvise or self-fund to bridge gaps.
9/9
STRUCT :: technology_gap
Absence of functioning digital infrastructure. Government-issued tablets are non-functional in most schools. Wi-Fi is absent in rural schools. Teachers use personal smartphones and personal data at own expense.
8/9
STRUCT :: retooling_inadequacy
Insufficient depth, coverage, and continuity of CBE teacher professional development. T05 estimates ≈20% effective retooling after nearly 10 years. Some senior school teachers have received no training.
6/9
STRUCT :: assessment_pressure
Persistence of high-stakes examination logic (KJSEA) alongside CBE's formative/competency rhetoric. Creates a de facto contradiction that pushes teachers back toward examination drilling.
8/9
STRUCT :: clustering_contradiction
The cluster school system (C1–C4) reproduces merit-based stratification under new labels, contradicting CBE's anti-ranking stance. Rural C4 schools often lack facilities, defeating the pathway placement logic.
5/9
STRUCT :: social_worker_absence
No professional social work function exists in Kenyan public schools. Administration absorbs this role informally, creating gaps in school-family-community coordination.
2/9
STRUCT :: teacher_wellbeing
Mental health impacts of workload, paperwork, emotional labour, and inadequate support. T01 describes a dedicated staff TPD session on stress management. Burnout risk is systemic.
3/9
STRUCT :: leadership_variability
Head teacher stance is a primary enabler or barrier of inclusive practice. T04 contrasts a previous obstructive head with a current supportive one; T08 identifies leadership renewal as the single most important change needed.
6/9

3/9
COLLAB :: parental_engagement
School-family interaction for learner support. Highly variable: supportive in urban schools, resistant or absent in rural/low-income contexts. Parental capacity-building meetings emerging but unevenly adopted.
8/9
COLLAB :: sne_as_resource
General education teachers consulting SNE specialists (within or outside school) for guidance on learner identification and adaptation strategies. Access to SNE expertise is highly context-dependent.
4/9
COLLAB :: kicd_tsc_retooling
Formal professional development delivered by KICD and TSC. Valued when attended, but coverage is incomplete (T05: ~20% effectiveness), and materials do not always match classroom realities.
7/9
5 — Teacher Agency & Moral Vocation
5 codes
AGENCY :: child_first_philosophy
Persistent learner-centred motivation that overrides institutional constraints, exhaustion, and lack of resources. Functions as the core moral driver of adaptive behaviour.
9/9
AGENCY :: personal_financial_investment
Teachers spending personal funds on classroom materials, printing, data bundles, tools (T04 bought a hammer for carpentry lesson). SNE allowance (Ksh 10,000) often reinvested in learners.
8/9
AGENCY :: vocation_heart
"Ni moyo" (it is the heart) — teaching as spiritual/moral calling. Strongest in SNE context (T04) but present across all participants as a legitimating narrative for going beyond professional obligation.
7/9
AGENCY :: unpaid_extra_time
Teachers arriving early, staying late, coming in on weekends, giving lunch-time tutorials — all without compensation. Legally discouraged extra tuition co-exists with this informal after-hours support.
8/9
AGENCY :: resilience_under_constraint
Active improvisation and workaround generation (field trips to workshops, chicken coop construction, downloading videos from cyber cafés) in the face of structural absence.
9/9
6 — Neurodivergence Recognition
5 codes
NEURO :: identification_no_diagnosis
Teachers recognise and name neurodivergent profiles (autism, ADHD, dyslexia) in the absence of formal diagnostic support. Identification is through sustained observation, not clinical assessment.
7/9
NEURO :: strength_based_framing
Spontaneous teacher attention to what a learner does well, as a diagnostic and motivational strategy. Art, music, sports, oral fluency, memory, and practical skills emerge as competency domains for learners who struggle with text.
6/9
NEURO :: parent_denial
Parental refusal to accept SEN identification, attributed to stigma, misinformation, and preference for mainstream placement. Creates a systemic barrier to referral and support.
5/9
NEURO :: medical_model_referral
Formal referral pathway: teacher → head → parent → hospital (medical assessment) → CSO (educational assessment) → placement. Slow, bureaucratic, and rarely completed in practice.
3/9
NEURO :: labelling_effects
Awareness of how labels (ADHD, "slow learner", "difficult child") follow learners between classes and schools, shaping teacher expectations and peer relations. T01 articulates this explicitly in relation to ADHD.
4/9
7 — CBE Paradox
5 codes
CBE :: aspirational_vs_enacted
Gap between CBE's published inclusive aspirations and its classroom-level reality. Present in all nine interviews in various forms.
9/9
CBE :: hybrid_system
CBE contains substantial 8-4-4 logic — assessment pressure, content overload, and merit-based placement — creating an ambiguous pedagogical space. T01's term "hybrid" captures this precisely.
7/9
CBE :: pathway_promise_vs_reality
The talent/competency pathway system is endorsed in principle but non-functional in most rural contexts where designated pathway schools lack facilities, teachers, and even adequate enrolment.
4/9
CBE :: parental_8-4-4_mindset
Parents' residual examination-oriented expectations conflict with CBE's project-based and competency-oriented demands. Creates friction around materials, grading transparency, and school choice.
6/9
CBE :: goodwill_despite_constraints
All teachers express genuine belief in CBE's potential despite enumerated failures. This creates a distinctive double-consciousness: systemic critique coexisting with personal commitment to the curriculum's vision.

Theme Coverage by School Context

Analytic Memos: Matrix Patterns

T1 (Adaptation Ensembles) — Universal: every teacher regardless of context assembles multi-strategy ensembles. Rural teachers rely more heavily on practical/hands-on and translanguaging; urban teachers have broader ICT access.
T3 (Structural Tensions) — Strongest in rural and SNE contexts (T04, T05, T08). Urban teachers experience it differently: they have more infrastructure but still face assessment pressure and class size issues.
T4 (Collaboration) — Co-teaching model (T07, T09) is an outlier that substantially elevates collaboration depth. Most other schools operate in informal lateral-only collaboration modes.
T5 (Teacher Agency) — Universal motivational substrate, but sustainability is uncertain. Notably highest in longest-serving teachers (T01, T07) and SNE specialist (T04).

Visualising the relationships between themes, the two Research Questions, and the conceptual construct of Inclusive Utility. Arrows indicate analytical connections; dashed arrows indicate enabling/constraining relationships.

Theoretical Integration Note

The thematic map illustrates how Inclusive Utility (3P) functions as the central analytical construct, receiving inputs from all seven themes. Theme 1 (Adaptation Ensembles) and Theme 5 (Teacher Agency) are the primary positive drivers of inclusive utility at the classroom level (microsystem). Theme 3 (Structural Tensions) constrains the feasible frontier from the exo- and macrosystem levels, while Theme 4 (Collaboration) functions as a partial compensatory mechanism — shifting the efficient frontier upward within the constraints set by Theme 3. Themes 6 and 7 (Neurodivergence Recognition and the CBE Paradox) operate as cross-cutting analytical lenses that colour all other themes, revealing the normative and political dimensions of what might otherwise appear as purely pedagogical decisions.
